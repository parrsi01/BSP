# Music Theory for House Producers

---

## Common House Music Keys

| Key | Type | Emotional Quality | Common Subgenre Use |
|-----|------|-----------------|-------------------|
| F minor | Minor | Dark, emotional, melancholic | Deep house, melodic house |
| A minor | Minor | Warm dark, accessible | All subgenres, most common |
| C minor | Minor | Powerful, dramatic | Soulful house, afro house |
| G minor | Minor | Rich, complex | Deep house, classic house |
| D minor | Minor | Somber, introspective | Melodic house, late night sets |
| C major | Major | Bright, uplifting | Soulful house, daytime energy |
| F major | Major | Warm, open | Soulful house, summer vibes |
| A major | Major | Bright, energetic | Big room house, festival energy |

**Why minor keys dominate house:** House music is rooted in soul and gospel but filtered through electronic darkness. Minor keys give house its emotional complexity — joy and melancholy coexisting on the dancefloor.

## Chord Progressions Used in House

### Minor Key Progressions

**i - VI - III - VII (Natural Minor)**
- Example in A minor: **Am - F - C - G**
- The most common house progression. Loops naturally. Every chord has forward momentum.
- Found in: virtually every subgenre

**i7 - iv7 (Minor 7th Loop)**
- Example: **Am7 - Dm7**
- Two bars each, repeating. Jazz influence. The 7th adds warmth.
- Found in: deep house, classic house

**i - bVII (Minimal Loop)**
- Example: **Am - G**
- Hypnotic, repetitive. Works well as an ostinato under a DJ set.
- Found in: minimal house, tech house, Balearic

**i - VII - VI - VII (Anthemic)**
- Example: **Am - G - F - G**
- Four chords, builds energy. Very usable for drops.
- Found in: commercial house, festival house

### Major Key Progressions

**I - IV - V - IV (Soulful Major)**
- Example in C major: **C - F - G - F**
- Gospel and soul influence. Bright and uplifting.
- Found in: soulful house, gospel house, NuDisco

**Imaj7 - IVmaj7 (Jazz Major)**
- Example: **Cmaj7 - Fmaj7**
- Sophisticated and lush. The major 7th interval adds elegance.
- Found in: deep house, lounge house, nu-jazz influenced

**I - V - vi - IV (Pop-House crossover)**
- Example: **C - G - Am - F**
- Very recognizable. Used in crossover house that reaches mainstream audiences.
- Found in: UK house, commercial house

### Jazz-Influenced Deep House

**ii7 - V7 - Imaj7 (Jazz II-V-I)**
- Example in C: **Dm7 - G7 - Cmaj7**
- Full jazz cadence. Sophisticated harmonic resolution.
- Used for: melodic bass lines that follow the harmony

**i9 - IV9 Substitution**
- Playing 9th chords: add the 2nd degree to the 7th chord
- Example Am9: A - C - E - G - B
- Adds color and texture without changing the harmonic function

## Chord Voicings for House

How you voice the chord (which octave each note is in) determines the energy and the mix position.

### Closed vs Open Voicing
- **Closed voicing:** All notes within one octave (e.g., C-E-G all in octave 4) — dense, compact
- **Open voicing:** Notes spread across 2+ octaves (e.g., C3-G3-E4-C5) — airy, spacious

**For house music:** Open voicings in the mid-high range (C4–C6) keep the low end clear for kick and bass.

### Classic House Voicing Pattern
Play the root in the bass register (octave 2–3) and the chord tones above (octave 4–5):
```
Bass note: A2 (the root)
Chord: C4 - E4 - G4 (the Am chord without doubling the root)
```
This gives the bass and pad their own frequency space without competing.

## Bassline Theory

### Root Note Bassline
The simplest house bassline: play the root of each chord on beat 1.
- Am section → A
- F section → F
- C section → C
- G section → G

### Octave Walk Bassline
Move between the root note and its octave (one octave up). Classic deep house technique.
```
Bar 1: A2 (beat 1) → A3 (beat 3)
Bar 2: A2 (beat 1) → A3 (beat 3) with passing note G3 on beat 4
```

### Chromatic Passing Notes
Add half-step movement between notes for a "walking" feel:
- Moving from Am root (A) to Dm root (D): A → Ab → G → F# → ... → D
- The chromatic descent creates forward motion and implies jazz harmony

### The "Skip" Pattern (tech house)
Repetitive rhythmic pattern on a single note, with occasional octave leaps:
```
A2 A2 A2 A3 A2 A2 A2 A3 (repeating)
```
Syncopated and mechanical — very common in tech house.

## Scales Used in House

### Natural Minor (Aeolian Mode)
- Formula: W H W W H W W
- A natural minor: A B C D E F G A
- The default "dark house" scale
- Dark and emotional — the foundation of most house music

### Dorian Mode (for Deep House Feel)
- Formula: W H W W W H W
- A Dorian: A B C D E F# G A
- **The difference from natural minor:** raised 6th degree (F# instead of F)
- The raised 6th gives Dorian a "sweeter" quality than natural minor
- Used heavily in: jazz-influenced deep house, Latin house, modal jazz-influenced progressions
- Try it: play Am in the melody but use F# instead of F — the change is subtle but profound

### Mixolydian Mode (for uplifting/major-flavored house)
- Formula: W W H W W H W
- A Mixolydian: A B C# D E F# G A
- Major scale but with a flattened 7th
- Creates an uplifting but slightly ambiguous feel — not as resolved as pure major
- Common in: UK garage, NuDisco, uplifting house

### Pentatonic Minor
- A minor pentatonic: A C D E G
- Remove the 2nd and 6th degree from natural minor
- Extremely simple to write melodies over — everything sounds "right"
- Used in: gospel house, soulful house melody lines, bass walks

## Melody Writing Over House Chords

### Call and Response
House melody is conversational: a 2-bar phrase followed by 2 bars of space.
- **Call (bars 1–2):** Play a 4–6 note melodic phrase
- **Response (bars 3–4):** Silence, or a short answering phrase
- This breathing space is essential — house is as much about the space as the notes

### Landing Notes
Start and end your melodic phrases on **chord tones** (root, 3rd, 5th of the current chord). Pass through other notes on the way. This technique works regardless of scale and sounds musical immediately.

### Melodic Rhythm (more important than the notes)
In house music, rhythm is the melody. A repeated note with a rhythmic hook will often sound more compelling than a complex melodic phrase with awkward rhythm.
- Try: the same 3-note idea, varied rhythmically over 8 bars
- Syncopated entries (starting on the "and" of a beat) create groove

## Tension and Release: Building a Drop with Suspended Chords

### Suspended Chords
A suspended chord replaces the 3rd with either the 2nd (sus2) or 4th (sus4):
- **Asus4:** A - D - E (the D replaces the C)
- **Asus2:** A - B - E (the B replaces the C)

Suspended chords have no major or minor quality — they feel **unresolved**, creating tension.

### Using Sus Chords in Builds
At the end of your 8-bar build (the last 2 beats before the drop):
1. Play the dominant chord (the V chord) as a suspended chord (e.g., Esus4 in A minor)
2. Hold it for 2 beats, building reverb and pressure
3. **The drop resolves to Am** — the listener's ear releases tension with relief

### Drop Resolution Formula
```
...Build → [Esus4 → silence] → DROP: Am with full arrangement
```

This resolution is so powerful it's used in virtually every major house track before a drop.

## Transposition for DJ Mixing (Harmonic Mixing)

DJs use **harmonic mixing** to transition between tracks whose keys are harmonically compatible. Understanding this helps you label and tag your beats correctly.

### Camelot Wheel (Simplified)

The Camelot wheel maps musical keys to numbers + letters (A = minor, B = major):

| Key | Camelot | Compatible Keys |
|-----|---------|----------------|
| A minor | 8A | 7A, 9A, 8B |
| D minor | 7A | 6A, 8A, 7B |
| G minor | 6A | 5A, 7A, 6B |
| C minor | 5A | 4A, 6A, 5B |
| F minor | 4A | 3A, 5A, 4B |
| Am adjacent (A major) | 11B | 10B, 12B, 11A |
| C major | 8B | 7B, 9B, 8A |
| F major | 7B | 6B, 8B, 7A |

### Why This Matters for Beat Sellers
- Tag your beats with the **key** on BeatStars — DJs use this to harmonic mix
- Producing beats in DJ-compatible keys (A minor, D minor, G minor cluster) increases their usefulness for DJ sets

## BPM Ranges by Subgenre

| Subgenre | BPM Range | Notes |
|---------|----------|-------|
| Deep House | 120–124 | Laid-back, jazz-influenced groove |
| Soulful House | 120–126 | Gospel and soul influence |
| Afro House | 118–124 | Polyrhythmic percussion, African influence |
| Classic / Chicago House | 120–130 | Original house music tempo range |
| UK Garage / Speed Garage | 130–136 | Faster, more syncopated |
| Soulful / Gospel House | 120–126 | Vocal-driven, church influence |
| Nu-Disco | 116–124 | Disco revival, slightly slower |
| Tech House | 126–132 | Driving, minimal, loop-based |
| Minimal House | 126–130 | Repetitive, textured, psychedelic |
| Progressive House | 128–132 | Melodic, builds over long periods |
| Electronic / Big Room House | 128–135 | Festival energy, anthemic |
| Micro House | 120–128 | Glitchy, experimental |

**Tip:** When producing for a specific DJ or event, research what BPM range they play. Sending a 130 BPM beat to a DJ who plays 122 BPM deep house is a mismatch.
