# Sample Clearance & Legal Use — Complete Guide

Everything a producer team needs to know about using samples legally, clearing them, and protecting the catalog from copyright claims.

---

## 1. What Is Sample Clearance?

Sample clearance is the legal process of obtaining permission from the copyright holders of recorded music before you use any portion of their work in your own production.

**Why it matters:**
- Using an uncleared sample in a commercial release is copyright infringement
- Infringement can result in: takedowns (DMCA), loss of revenue, injunctions against further distribution, lawsuits, and statutory damages up to $150,000 per willful infringement in the US
- Music distributors (DistroKid, TuneCore) will remove your content and may ban your account
- Streaming platforms will remove monetization and flag your catalog
- If an artist buys your beat and it contains an uncleared sample, you are liable — and the artist may sue you for selling them a defective product

**Real cases that established the precedent:**
- *Bridgeport Music v. Dimension Films (2004)*: Even a 2-second guitar sample required clearance. "Get a license or do not sample."
- *Campbell v. Acuff-Rose (1994)*: 2 Live Crew parody case — established fair use applies, but it is narrow and must be argued in court, which is expensive even when you win.

---

## 2. Types of Samples That Need Clearance

### Sound Recording (Master)
Using any audio from an existing commercial recording — even one note, one drum hit, one vocal clip — requires clearance from the master rights holder (usually the label or the artist if independent). This includes:
- Loops from commercial records
- Vocal chops taken from released songs
- Drum breaks lifted from albums
- Instrumentals sampled from other producers' beats

### Melody / Composition (Even If Re-Recorded)
If you replay (interpolate) a melody or chord progression that is substantially similar to a copyrighted composition, you need clearance for the underlying composition — even though no recorded audio was used. You only need one clearance (the publishing/composition), not the master, but you still need it.

**Example:** If you replay the bass line from a recognizable song note-for-note on your keyboard, you need composition clearance.

### Drum Breaks — The "Amen Break" Problem

The Amen Break (from The Winstons' "Amen, Brother," 1969) is the most sampled break in music history. The composition copyright is not registered, and the sound recording copyright status is contested. It is widely used without clearance, but:
- The sound recording copyright may still be claimed depending on the recording date and renewal status
- A copyright holder can file a claim at any time
- "Everyone samples it" is not a legal defense

**Rule:** If a drum break comes from a named commercial release, treat it as potentially clearance-required.

## 3. When You DON'T Need Clearance

| Source | Clearance Needed? | Notes |
|--------|-------------------|-------|
| Sounds you recorded yourself | No | You own the original |
| Sounds you synthesized from scratch | No | Original creation |
| Royalty-free samples (properly licensed) | No | Check the license terms carefully |
| CC0 (public domain) samples | No | Must be verifiably CC0 — document it |
| Public domain recordings (US pre-1928) | Generally No | See public domain rules below |
| Pure drum patterns / rhythms | No | Patterns and rhythms are not copyrightable — only specific fixed recordings |
| Facts, scale runs, basic chord progressions | No | Not copyrightable as such |
| Purchased sample packs from legitimate vendors | No | Covered by vendor's license — keep receipts |

### Public Domain Rules (US)

| Recording Date | Status (as of 2024) |
|----------------|---------------------|
| Before January 1, 1928 | Public domain |
| 1928–1972 | State law applies; many not yet PD federally |
| 1972–2002 | Protected for 95 years from publication or 2067, whichever is later |
| Post-1972 (general) | Standard copyright term applies |

**Caution:** Even if a composition is in the public domain, a specific recording of it may be protected (different copyrights). A 1923 song in public domain — if you sample a 1985 recording of it, the recording is not public domain.

## 4. How to Clear a Sample — Step by Step

### Step 1: Identify the Rights Holders

You need to clear **two separate rights** for any sampled recording:
1. **Master rights** (the sound recording) — held by the record label or the independent artist
2. **Publishing rights** (the composition) — held by the publisher or the songwriter/composer

These can be different companies entirely. Both must be cleared independently.

**How to find rights holders:**
- Spotify/Apple Music credits in the track listing
- ASCAP database (ascap.com) — search by song title to find publishers
- BMI Works database (bmi.com/repertoire) — same
- AllMusic or Discogs for label information
- SoundExchange for digital performance rights holders

### Step 2: Find Contact Information

| Rights Type | Where to Find Contact |
|------------|----------------------|
| Master (major label) | Label's licensing department — check the label website |
| Master (independent) | Artist's management or direct email |
| Publishing (major publisher) | Publisher's licensing/permissions department website |
| Publishing (self-published) | PRO database → contact writer directly |
| Harry Fox Agency | Mechanical licensing for specific US publishers (harryfox.com) |

### Step 3: Sample Clearance Request Letter — Template

```
Subject: Sample Clearance Request — [Your Project Title]

To [Rights Holder / Licensing Department],

My name is [Your Name], representing [BSP / team name],
a music production company.

We are requesting a license to use a sample from the following recording
in our upcoming project:

Original Work:
- Title: [Song Title]
- Artist: [Recording Artist]
- Album: [Album Name]
- Label: [Label]
- Release Year: [Year]
- Record Label Catalog #: [if known]

Sample Description:
- Time position: [e.g., 0:32–0:36]
- Duration used: [e.g., 4 seconds]
- How it will be used: [loop, one-shot, melodic interpolation]

Our Project:
- Title: [Your Beat/Track Title]
- Intended release format: [beat tape, artist album, single]
- Distribution: [DistroKid / BeatStars / etc.]
- Anticipated release date: [date]
- Projected distribution: [estimated streams, physical units]

We are happy to discuss licensing fees and terms.
Please advise on your clearance process.

Best regards,
[Your Name]
[Email] | [Phone]
[Website / BeatStars link]
```

### Step 4: Negotiate the Fee

Sample fees are not standardized. They are negotiated based on:
- How prominent the sample is (lead element vs background)
- Length of sample used
- How famous the original artist/song is
- Anticipated commercial scope of your release
- Whether you want a flat fee or royalty split

**Flat fee:** You pay once; no further royalties owed. Better for you long-term if the release succeeds.

**Royalty split:** Lower or no upfront fee, but rights holder gets a percentage of future royalties. Their percentage is deducted from your publishing and/or master royalties.

## 6. Royalty-Free Alternatives to Sampling

Instead of sampling, consider:

**Replaying (Re-recording):**
Record the melodic or harmonic elements yourself using VSTs or live instruments. This eliminates the master rights clearance requirement. You still need composition clearance if you are reproducing a copyrighted melody closely — but if you write your own variation inspired by (not copied from) the original, no clearance is needed.

**Interpolation:**
Rewrite the melody, chords, or rhythm to be substantially similar but legally distinct. There is no bright line test — courts use "substantial similarity" standards. Get at least 3–4 elements changed if you are taking inspiration from an existing song.

**From scratch:**
The safest and most creatively valuable approach. Build your own sample by:
- Recording your own instruments (even simple piano sketches)
- Synthesizing all sounds from VSTs
- Using legally cleared sample packs with commercial licenses

---

## 7. Interpolation vs Sampling — Legal Difference

| | Sampling | Interpolation |
|--|---------|---------------|
| Definition | Using the actual audio recording | Re-recording a melody or element yourself |
| Master clearance needed | Yes | No |
| Publishing clearance needed | Yes (if melody/composition used) | Yes (if melody substantially similar) |
| Risk level | High (exact copy) | Lower (if sufficiently transformed) |
| Cost | Higher | Lower (just publishing, if needed at all) |

**Practical example:** Instead of sampling the bass line from a classic record, replay it on a synth. You now only need publishing clearance (if the bass line is a copyrightable original element of the song), not master clearance.

## 9. What "Royalty-Free" Actually Means

**Common misconception:** "Royalty-free" means free to use for free.

**What it actually means:** You pay once (for the sample pack, subscription, or license) and do not owe ongoing per-use royalties afterward. You still pay upfront.

**What royalty-free does NOT give you:**
- Ownership of the sample
- Exclusive use rights (others can also buy and use the same samples)
- The right to resell the sample as part of your own sample pack
- Protection from DMCA if you use the same pack as another track that gets flagged

**Always read the specific license:** Common restrictions in "royalty-free" packs:
- Cannot be used in sample packs or loop libraries
- Cannot be used in NFTs (on some platforms)
- Non-commercial use only (on free tiers)
- Attribution required (CC-BY)

## 10. Creative Commons Licenses Explained

| License | What You Can Do | Commercial Use | Share-Alike Required | Attribution Required |
|---------|----------------|----------------|---------------------|---------------------|
| CC0 (Public Domain Dedication) | Anything, no restrictions | Yes | No | No |
| CC-BY | Use, adapt, distribute | Yes | No | Yes |
| CC-BY-SA | Use, adapt — share under same license | Yes | Yes | Yes |
| CC-BY-NC | Use, adapt — non-commercial only | No | No | Yes |
| CC-BY-NC-SA | Non-commercial + share-alike | No | Yes | Yes |
| CC-BY-ND | Use as-is only, no derivatives | Yes | N/A | Yes |
| CC-BY-NC-ND | Most restrictive | No | N/A | Yes |

**For producers:** Only CC0 and CC-BY are reliably safe for commercial beat production. CC-BY-SA may create issues (your beat would need to be released under the same license). CC-BY-NC is explicitly not for commercial use.

**Where to find CC0 audio:** Freesound.org (filter by CC0), ccMixter (filter by CC0), NASA audio archives.

## 11. Risk Management — How to Assess Sample Risk Before Uploading or Selling

Use this checklist before adding any sample to a beat you intend to sell or distribute:

### Sample Risk Assessment Checklist

```
[ ] Source identified: where did this sample come from?
[ ] License documented: do I have the license file or email confirming use?
[ ] Commercial use confirmed: does the license explicitly allow commercial use?
[ ] Resale check: does the license allow use in beats sold to others?
[ ] Original recording check: is any portion from a commercial release?
[ ] Recognizability test: would an average listener identify the original source?
[ ] Publishing check: if melody used, is it original or derived from a known song?
[ ] Platform compliance: does the license comply with BeatStars/SoundCloud/DistroKid TOS?
```

### Risk Level Matrix

| Sample Type | Risk Level | Action |
|------------|-----------|--------|
| Fully original, self-recorded/synthesized | None | Safe to use |
| Licensed sample pack (commercial tier, receipt kept) | Very Low | Document license, proceed |
| CC0 sample from verified source | Very Low | Verify, document, proceed |
| CC-BY sample | Low | Add attribution in metadata |
| CC-BY-NC sample | High | Do not use in commercial releases |
| Sample from a commercial release (uncleared) | Critical | Do not use |
| "Found" sample from YouTube, SoundCloud, etc. | Critical | Do not use |
| Drum break from a named commercial recording | High–Critical | Do not use without clearance |

### If You Are Already Selling a Beat with an Uncleared Sample

1. Remove it from your store immediately.
2. Notify any buyers who leased it — provide an alternate beat or refund.
3. Remove the tagged preview from SoundCloud.
4. If it was distributed to streaming platforms, take it down via your distributor.
5. Do not wait for a claim — proactive removal reduces liability.

### Content ID and Sample Risk

YouTube's Content ID system will automatically detect audio matches against registered recordings. If your beat contains a sample, even tiny, and that recording is registered with Content ID:
- Your YouTube upload will be claimed
- Revenue will be redirected to the rights holder
- Your BeatStars/DistroKid Content ID service (if enrolled) will have conflicts
- Artists who bought your beat may have their YouTube videos claimed — damaging your reputation

**Prevention:** Only enroll beats in Content ID if they contain zero uncleared samples. Keep a list of which beats are "sync-safe" and which are not.
