# Music Business 101 — What Every Producer Must Know

A working reference for the business side of beat production. No law degree required — just enough to protect your work and get paid correctly.

---

## 1. Copyright Basics

### Two Separate Copyrights in Every Song

| Copyright Type | What It Covers | Who Holds It |
|----------------|---------------|--------------|
| Sound Recording (Master) | The actual recorded audio file — the MP3/WAV you made | Usually the producer or label |
| Musical Composition | The underlying melody, harmony, and lyrics | Songwriter / composer (you) |

When you make a beat, you automatically hold **both** copyrights the moment it is "fixed in a tangible medium" — i.e., the moment you save the file. No registration required for copyright to exist. Registration is required to enforce it in federal court.

### Who Owns What When You Sell a Beat

**Non-exclusive lease:** You retain both copyrights. The buyer gets a limited license to use the beat. You can keep selling it.

**Exclusive sale:** You transfer the master rights (sound recording) to the buyer. You can negotiate to retain the publishing/composition copyright — and you should. Many producers retain publishing even on exclusive sales. If your contract is silent on publishing, negotiate it explicitly.

**Work-for-hire:** The buyer owns the copyright entirely — composition and master — from the moment of creation. This is the arrangement when a producer is paid as a contractor by a label or major artist to produce content as part of employment or a specific contract. Avoid work-for-hire arrangements unless the upside is significant, because you give up all future royalties.

### Copyright Registration

**Why register?** Unregistered copyright still exists, but you cannot sue for statutory damages or attorney's fees in US federal court without it. If someone steals your beat and you have no registration, your practical recourse is limited.

**Process (US Copyright Office):**
1. Go to copyright.gov/registration
2. Create an account
3. File Form SR (Sound Recording) for the master and Form PA (Performing Arts) for the composition — or file both with a single SR application covering your own composition
4. Upload your audio file
5. Pay the filing fee ($35–$65 per work for online filing; group registrations of unpublished works possible for one fee)
6. Allow 3–8 months for processing

**Cost-saving tip:** You can register a collection of unpublished works (e.g., 10 beats not yet released) as a "collection" under one application for one fee. Once published (uploaded publicly or sold), each needs its own registration.

**When to register:** Before you release or sell any beat. Ideally, register in batches quarterly.

## 2. Publishing

### What Publishing Rights Are

"Publishing" refers to the musical composition copyright — the underlying song (melody + chords + structure). The "publisher" is whoever administers those rights and collects money when the composition is used.

As a producer, you are both the composer AND can be your own publisher. You do not need an external publishing company to collect your publishing income — you can collect directly through a Performing Rights Organization (PRO).

### PROs: ASCAP vs BMI vs SESAC

| | ASCAP | BMI | SESAC |
|--|-------|-----|-------|
| Type | Non-profit (member-owned) | Non-profit (broadcaster-owned) | For-profit (private) |
| Sign-up fee | $50 one-time (writer) | Free | Invitation only |
| Publisher registration | $150 one-time | Free | Invitation only |
| Royalty distribution | Quarterly | Quarterly | Quarterly |
| Best for producers | Yes (open to all) | Yes (open to all, free) | Established acts only |
| International collection | Yes (via reciprocal deals) | Yes | Yes |

**Which to join:** ASCAP or BMI — both are equally functional for producers. BMI is free to join as a writer. ASCAP costs $50. Many producers choose BMI to start. You can only be a member of one US PRO at a time.

**Also register a publishing entity:** To collect the publisher's share (50% of performance royalties), you must register a publishing company name with your PRO. This can be your team name (e.g., "BSP Publishing"). BMI and ASCAP both let you register a publishing affiliate at the same time you register as a writer.

### How to Register Your Beats with a PRO

1. Join ASCAP or BMI as a writer.
2. Register your publishing entity with the same PRO.
3. For each beat: log in and submit a "work registration" — title, ISWC (if assigned), writers (you and any collaborators), publisher, and percentage splits.
4. Do this before distribution or before any beat is sold.

### Performance Royalties — How They Work

When a beat is played publicly — on radio, in a club, at a venue, on streaming services, in a TV show — the PRO collects a fee from the broadcaster or platform and distributes it to registered writers and publishers.

- **Writer's share:** 50% of performance royalties paid to you as the songwriter/composer
- **Publisher's share:** 50% paid to your registered publishing entity (which is also you, if you registered a publisher affiliate)
- Combined, you collect 100% of performance royalties as a self-published producer

**Important:** You must be registered with a PRO to receive these payments. Unregistered works generate royalties that accumulate in a "black box" pool — often redistributed to other registered writers.

### Mechanical Royalties — What They Are and When They Apply

Mechanical royalties are paid when a composition is reproduced — on a physical recording (CD, vinyl), as a digital download, or via interactive streaming (Spotify, Apple Music).

**Rate (US statutory rate):** $0.091 per copy for songs under 5 minutes (physical/download). Streaming mechanical rates are negotiated per platform.

**When they apply to producers:** If you distribute beat tapes or instrumentals via DistroKid/TuneCore, your tracks generate mechanical royalties. These are collected by:
- The distributor (for digital)
- Harry Fox Agency / Music Reports (for physical)
- A publishing administrator (DistroKid's Publishing service, Songtrust, or CD Baby Pro)

**Action item:** Sign up for a publishing administrator service (Songtrust, TuneCore Publishing, or DistroKid's add-on) so mechanical royalties are automatically collected across all platforms. Cost: approximately $20–$100/year depending on service.

## 3. Licensing Fundamentals

### License vs Assignment

| | License | Assignment |
|--|---------|------------|
| What transfers | Right to use under conditions | Full ownership of the copyright |
| Reversible | Yes (on expiration or breach) | No |
| Who retains copyright | You (the producer) | The buyer |
| Beat leases | License | — |
| Exclusive sale (full) | — | Assignment |
| Exclusive sale (master only) | Partial assignment | — |

**Rule:** License whenever possible. Assign only when the fee justifies losing all future royalties.

### Non-Exclusive vs Exclusive Licensing

**Non-exclusive:** Multiple people can license the same beat simultaneously. You retain copyright and keep selling. This is the standard beat lease model.

**Exclusive:** One buyer gets sole rights to that beat (or the specific recording). You cannot license it to anyone else. Higher price, higher risk of leaving future royalties on the table if the beat blows up.

### Standard License Terms Every Lease Should Define

| Term | What It Means | Example |
|------|--------------|---------|
| Territory | Where the license applies | Worldwide |
| Term | How long the license lasts | Lifetime / 2 years |
| Distribution cap | Maximum streams or copies | 500,000 streams, 5,000 downloads |
| Allowed uses | What the buyer can do with it | Non-profit, commercial, radio |
| Credit requirement | How buyer must credit you | "Prod. By BSP" in title |
| Synchronization | Can they use it in video | Specify yes/no |
| Sublicensing | Can they license it further | Usually no |
| Termination clause | What voids the license | Non-payment, contract breach |

### For Profit vs Non-Commercial

Most standard leases are for commercial use (the artist intends to sell or monetize the music). Some artists request non-commercial rates (for school projects, non-profit use). You can offer these at lower prices — just define "non-commercial" explicitly in the contract as "no revenue generation, no streaming monetization, no digital sales."

### Standard Beat License Clauses Every Producer Should Include

1. **Grant of License:** Clearly states the buyer receives a non-exclusive license for the specified uses only.
2. **Delivery:** Specifies which files are delivered (MP3 only, or WAV + trackout, etc.).
3. **Credit:** "Music produced by [BSP / your tag]" must appear in all recordings.
4. **Distribution Cap:** Numeric limit on streams/sales beyond which a new license is required.
5. **Prohibited Uses:** Cannot resell the beat, lease it to others, or register it with a PRO as a composition owner.
6. **Termination:** License terminates if buyer fails to credit or exceeds distribution cap.
7. **Governing Law:** Specify jurisdiction (e.g., state/country).
8. **Entire Agreement:** This document supersedes all verbal agreements.

## 4. Master vs Publishing Split

### What Transfers on a Beat Sale

| Sale Type | Master Rights | Publishing (Composition) |
|-----------|--------------|--------------------------|
| Non-exclusive lease | Stays with producer | Stays with producer |
| Exclusive (master only) | Transfers to buyer | Stays with producer |
| Exclusive (full buyout) | Transfers | Transfers |
| Work-for-hire | Transfers | Transfers |

### Why Producers Should Always Retain Publishing

Publishing is where long-term royalties come from — streaming mechanicals, sync placements, radio play. If you sell an exclusive beat and transfer both master and publishing, you receive the sale price and nothing more. If that song gets placed in a Netflix show 5 years later, the publisher collects $10,000 — and that's no longer you.

**Standard practice for exclusive beat sales:** Transfer master rights, retain 50% of publishing (your writer's share + publisher's share). This is negotiable and some artists will push back, but it is industry standard to retain at least the writer's share.

### The 50/50 Publishing Split Explained

In major label contracts, publishing is often split 50/50 between the songwriter and the publisher. This originated with traditional publishers who administered, pitched, and collected for songs. As a self-published producer, you collect both halves — but if you co-write with an artist who has a traditional publisher, their publisher will claim 50% of the composition.

**Implication for producers:** Always clarify publishing ownership in every exclusive sale contract. "50% publishing retained by producer" must be in writing or it may not be enforceable.

## 5. Taxes for Producers

### Self-Employment Income

Beat sales, commissions, streaming payouts, and services (mixing, mastering) are all self-employment income. You are responsible for:
- Income tax (federal + state)
- Self-employment tax (15.3% on net profit — covers Social Security and Medicare)

### Trackable Deductible Expenses

| Category | Examples |
|----------|---------|
| Equipment | MIDI controllers, audio interfaces, microphones, monitors, headphones, hard drives |
| Software | DAW licenses, plugin subscriptions (Splice), sample packs, VSTs |
| Studio space | Home studio % of rent/mortgage (home office deduction), rented studio time |
| Internet | Portion attributable to business use |
| Education | Courses, masterclasses, books on music/business |
| Marketing | Ad spend, domain, website hosting, BeatStars plan |
| Professional services | Accountant fees, legal contracts |
| Music services | DistroKid, TuneCore, PRO registration fees, Copyright Office fees |

**Track every expense from day one.** Use a dedicated bank account or credit card for business purchases.

### Quarterly Estimated Taxes

If you expect to owe $1,000 or more in taxes this year, the IRS requires quarterly estimated payments:
- Due dates: April 15, June 15, September 15, January 15 (following year)
- Calculate using IRS Form 1040-ES or a tax calculator
- Underpayment penalty applies if you pay less than 90% of owed taxes by year end

### When to Get an Accountant vs DIY

| Situation | DIY (TurboTax/FreeTaxUSA) | Accountant (CPA/EA) |
|-----------|--------------------------|---------------------|
| Single member, under $30K/year income | Yes | Optional |
| Multiple members, revenue from multiple streams | No | Yes |
| LLC or S-Corp elected | No | Yes |
| Sync licensing deals or international income | No | Yes |
| First year with significant business income | No | Yes (set up correctly from the start) |

## 6. Business Entities

### Sole Proprietor vs LLC

| | Sole Proprietor | LLC |
|--|-----------------|-----|
| Formation cost | None | $50–$500 state filing fee |
| Liability protection | None (personal liability for debts) | Limited (personal assets protected) |
| Tax treatment | Pass-through (Schedule C) | Pass-through by default; S-Corp election possible |
| Credibility | Lower | Higher (bank accounts, contracts) |
| Complexity | None | Low–Medium |

**Recommendation for BSP:** Form an LLC once you are generating consistent income ($500+/month) or before signing any significant contract or sync deal. The liability protection is worth the filing fee.

### How to Split a Business 4 Ways

Options for multi-member ownership:

1. **Multi-Member LLC:** Each member owns a percentage (e.g., 25% each). Requires an Operating Agreement. Taxed as a partnership — each member reports their share on personal taxes.

2. **General Partnership:** No formal filing required, but no liability protection. Not recommended.

3. **S-Corporation:** More complex, more tax efficiency at higher income levels. Requires LLC formation first, then S-Corp election via IRS Form 2553. Consider when total profit exceeds $80,000/year.

### Operating Agreement Basics

Even if your state does not require it, a multi-member LLC needs an Operating Agreement covering:
- Member percentages
- How profits/losses are distributed (can differ from ownership %)
- Decision-making: majority vote vs unanimous consent
- What happens when a member wants to leave (buyout clause)
- How to admit new members
- Dissolution process

## 7. Contracts Every Producer Needs

### Beat Lease Agreement — Template Outline

```
BEAT LEASE AGREEMENT

Date: ___________
Producer: [BSP / Producer name], ("Licensor")
Artist: ___________("Licensee")
Beat Title: ___________
BPM: ___   Key: ___

1. GRANT OF LICENSE
   Licensor grants Licensee a non-exclusive license to use the Beat
   for one (1) commercial sound recording under the following terms:

2. LICENSE TERMS
   - License Type: [Basic / Premium / Unlimited]
   - File Format Delivered: [MP3 / WAV / Trackout]
   - Distribution Cap: [X] streams, [X] downloads, [X] physical copies
   - Territory: Worldwide
   - Term: Lifetime (or [X] years)

3. CREDIT REQUIREMENT
   All recordings must credit: "Prod. by [BSP Tag]"

4. PROHIBITED USES
   Licensee may not: resell, sublicense, register as sole publisher,
   or use for sync without a separate sync license.

5. FEE
   One-time license fee: $_____ (non-refundable)

6. TERMINATION
   License terminates upon breach of any term herein.

Signatures: _____________ / _____________
```

### Exclusive Purchase Agreement — Template Outline

```
EXCLUSIVE BEAT SALE AGREEMENT

Date: ___________
Seller (Producer): [BSP]
Buyer (Artist): ___________
Beat Title: ___________

1. TRANSFER OF RIGHTS
   Seller transfers exclusive master rights to the Sound Recording.
   Seller RETAINS 50% publishing rights (writer's share).

2. PURCHASE PRICE
   One-time payment of $_____ (non-refundable).

3. FUTURE ROYALTIES
   Buyer is responsible for all distribution and streaming royalties
   on the master. Seller continues to collect publishing royalties
   from Seller's registered PRO.

4. CREDIT
   All releases must credit "Prod. by [BSP Tag]".

5. WARRANTY
   Seller warrants the beat is original and free of uncleared samples.

Signatures: _____________ / _____________
```

### Collab/Split Agreement — Template Outline

For beats made with another producer or writer:

```
COLLABORATION SPLIT AGREEMENT

Beat Title: ___________
Date: ___________

Contributors and splits:
- [Name 1]: ___% master / ___% publishing
- [Name 2]: ___% master / ___% publishing

Revenue distribution:
- All master revenue split per above percentages
- Each party registers their publishing share with their own PRO
- Decisions on licensing require majority written approval

Signatures: _____________ / _____________
```

### Ghost Production NDA — Key Clauses

A ghost production NDA must cover:
1. Confidentiality: Producer will not disclose involvement publicly
2. IP Transfer: All rights transfer to client upon full payment
3. Non-Attribution: Producer cannot claim public credit for the work
4. Non-Compete (optional): Scope and term of any restriction
5. Payment: Full payment required before rights transfer
