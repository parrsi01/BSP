# Beat Licensing Guide

Understanding licensing protects your income and prevents legal disputes.

---

## License Types Explained

### Non-Exclusive License (Lease)
- The **most common** license sold on BeatStars/Airbit
- You (the producer) retain ownership of the beat
- You can sell the **same beat to multiple artists** simultaneously
- The artist gets limited usage rights defined by the license terms

**Typical terms for a basic non-exclusive lease:**
- Up to 2,500 audio streams
- Up to 1,000 downloads
- 1 music video
- Non-profit/paid performance limited to 1–2 events

### Exclusive License
- The artist gets **sole rights** to use the beat commercially
- You **can no longer sell** that beat to other artists after the sale
- The artist may or may not receive the copyright (depends on your terms)
- **You typically retain the underlying composition copyright** (the melody, chords, arrangement) unless you explicitly sign it over

**What you should keep even in an exclusive:**
- Your producer credit (mandatory by default under copyright law)
- Performance royalties as the composer (via ASCAP/BMI)
- Mechanical royalties if you registered the underlying composition

### Unlimited License
- A non-exclusive license with **no usage caps** — unlimited streams, downloads, performances
- Priced between a standard lease and an exclusive ($100–$200 typical)
- You still retain the beat and can sell it to others

### License Tier Summary

| License | Price Range | Streams | Downloads | Video | Beat Still Sold? |
|---------|------------|---------|-----------|-------|-----------------|
| Basic MP3 | $19–$39 | 2,500 | 500 | 1 | Yes |
| Premium WAV | $49–$79 | 10,000 | 1,000 | 1 | Yes |
| Unlimited | $99–$149 | Unlimited | Unlimited | Unlimited | Yes |
| Exclusive | $200–$500+ | Unlimited | Unlimited | Unlimited | No |
| Exclusive + Stems | $500–$2,000+ | Unlimited | Unlimited | Unlimited | No |

## What Each License Covers

### Streaming Platforms (Spotify, Apple Music, Tidal)
- Covered by non-exclusive leases up to the stream limit
- Once the limit is reached, the artist must upgrade to a higher tier or exclusive
- **For viral releases:** artists often buy unlimited or exclusive upfront

### YouTube
- Non-exclusive covers "1 music video" typically
- If the artist uses your beat in multiple videos (vlogs, visualizers), they need unlimited
- You may still collect YouTube Content ID royalties even on leased beats — check your platform's terms

### Radio Play
- Most standard leases **do not include** radio rights
- Radio play rights are usually in the "Unlimited" or higher tier
- **Performance royalties:** Even if the artist has a lease, you earn performance royalties from ASCAP/BMI when your beat airs on radio — register the beat

### Live Performance
- Most leases limit to 1–2 paid events
- Artists performing your leased beat at sold-out venues should have upgraded
- Enforcement is difficult — clear terms reduce disputes

### TV, Film, and Commercials (Sync Licensing)
- **NOT covered by standard beat leases**
- Requires a separate **sync license** — typically negotiated per-project
- Much higher value: $500 to $50,000+ depending on the production

## License Agreement Templates

Rather than writing contracts from scratch, start with free templates and customize:

- **BeatStars built-in license:** When you sell through BeatStars, their standard license is auto-attached. Review and customize it in your BeatStars settings.
- **Airbit license:** Same — built-in license attached to every sale.
- **NOLO.com** — provides basic contract templates (US law)
- **Law Insider** ([lawinsider.com](https://www.lawinsider.com)) — free searchable contract database; search "beat license agreement"
- **Beatmaker Law** resources — search for producer-specific contract templates

**Key clauses your license should include:**
1. Licensee name and date
2. Beat title and your producer name
3. License type (non-exclusive/exclusive)
4. Usage caps (streams, downloads, performances)
5. Credit requirement: "Produced by [Your Name]"
6. Platforms covered
7. Prohibition on reselling or sublicensing the beat
8. Governing law (your state/country)
9. Dispute resolution

## Copyright Basics for Producers

### What You Automatically Own
When you create a beat, you have:
- **Copyright in the sound recording** (the audio file you produced)
- **Copyright in the musical composition** (the underlying melody, chords, rhythm)

Copyright exists from the moment of creation. You do **not** need to register to own it.

### What Registration Does
Registering with the Copyright Office (US) or equivalent:
- Creates a **public record** of your ownership
- Allows you to **sue for statutory damages** (up to $150,000 per infringement) — vs. only actual damages without registration
- **Required before filing an infringement lawsuit** in the US

### Samples and Clearances
- **Do not sample** copyrighted records without clearing the sample
- Free/royalty-free loops (Splice, Loopmasters, etc.) do NOT require clearance
- If you use a recognizable melody or drum loop from a copyrighted track — clear it or don't use it

## How to Register Beats with the US Copyright Office

### Online Registration — copyright.gov

1. Go to [copyright.gov](https://www.copyright.gov) → eCO (Electronic Copyright Office)
2. Create an account
3. Click **Register a New Claim**
4. **Type of Work:** Sound Recording (for the audio file)
5. **Title:** Your beat name
6. **Year of Creation:** The year you made it
7. **Author:** Your legal name (or business name if you have one)
8. **Claimant:** Same as author (unless you've transferred rights)
9. **Upload:** MP3 or WAV of the beat
10. **Fee:** $65 for single work online registration (2024 rates — check copyright.gov for current fees)
11. Allow 3–6 months for processing

**Tip:** Register multiple beats in one "group registration" to reduce per-beat cost. A collection of unpublished works can be registered as one claim.

## PRO Registration: ASCAP, BMI, SESAC

A **Performing Rights Organization (PRO)** collects performance royalties when your music is:
- Played on radio
- Streamed on platforms
- Performed live
- Used in TV/film

### Which to Join

| PRO | Cost | Best For |
|-----|------|---------|
| [ASCAP](https://www.ascap.com) | $50 one-time (US) | Most established; better for indie artists |
| [BMI](https://www.bmi.com) | Free for songwriters | Slightly better for electronic/beats |
| [SESAC](https://www.sesac.com) | Invite-only | Premium but selective |

**Choose one — you cannot belong to both ASCAP and BMI.** Most house producers use **BMI** (free to join).

### After Joining
1. Register each beat as a **work** in your PRO account
2. Include co-writer splits if you collaborated
3. Register your publishing company (even as a sole proprietor) for additional publishing royalties
4. PROs distribute royalties quarterly — expect a 6–12 month lag on first payments

## Mechanical Royalties Explained

Mechanical royalties are owed when someone **reproduces** your composition (makes copies — streaming counts):

- When an artist releases your beat on Spotify, **mechanical royalties** are generated
- The artist's distributor (DistroKid, etc.) pays mechanicals to a collection service
- **MLC (Mechanical Licensing Collective)** in the US collects and distributes these
- Register your works with your PRO, and the MLC will match them

**Rate (US, 2024):** $0.091 per physical/download sale; streaming rates are statutory and vary.

## Sync Licensing: TV, Film, Ads

### What Sync Licensing Is
A "sync license" is paid when your music is **synchronized** with visual media — a TV show, film, commercial, YouTube video, game, etc.

### Rates (General Reference)
| Placement | Fee Range |
|-----------|----------|
| Indie/student film | $50–$500 |
| Online ad (small brand) | $500–$3,000 |
| Network TV show | $2,000–$10,000 |
| National TV commercial | $10,000–$75,000+ |
| Major film | $5,000–$50,000+ |

### How to Pursue Sync Licensing
1. **Register with a sync licensing marketplace:**
   - [Musicbed](https://www.musicbed.com) — quality, curated
   - [Artlist](https://artlist.io) — subscription model
   - [Pond5](https://www.pond5.com) — large marketplace
   - [Songtradr](https://www.songtradr.com) — global sync marketplace
2. Submit instrumental versions of your beats (vocals complicate sync licensing)
3. Provide a **fully cleared beat** (no uncleared samples)
4. Create loop versions and alternate edits for placement flexibility

## Protecting Yourself Legally

### Before Every Sale
- [ ] Your beat is original or uses 100% cleared/royalty-free samples
- [ ] License agreement is clearly attached to the sale (BeatStars/Airbit does this automatically)
- [ ] Credit requirement ("Produced by [Your Name]") is written into the license

### Beat Tag Strategy
- Tag the preview file (MP3) — discourages unauthorized use
- Watermark does not replace a license, but it identifies the beat as yours publicly

### If Someone Uses Your Beat Without a License
1. Document the unauthorized use (screenshot, URL, date)
2. Send a **DMCA takedown notice** to the platform hosting the content
3. YouTube/Spotify/SoundCloud have DMCA portals — use them
4. If they claim they purchased a license — request proof (transaction ID from BeatStars/Airbit)
5. For large commercial infringements — consult a music attorney

### Keeping Records
- Save every transaction from BeatStars/Airbit (export monthly reports)
- Keep a spreadsheet: Beat Name | Platform | Buyer | License Type | Date | Amount
- Store your original project files (DAW sessions) as proof of creation date
