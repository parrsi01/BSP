# Building Artist Relationships — Long-Term Revenue Strategy

The difference between producers who make $50/month and producers who make $500+/month consistently is almost never the quality of their beats. It is the depth and number of artist relationships they have built.

One-time sales are transactional. Relationships generate repeat purchases, referrals, exclusives, and retainers. A single artist who loves your beats and releases music regularly is worth more than 50 random one-off buyers.

---

## 1. Finding the Right Artists

You want artists who are actively creating music, have enough of an audience to validate the investment, and are not already locked in with another producer.

### Platform-by-platform search criteria

**SoundCloud**

| Criteria | Target range | Why |
|----------|-------------|-----|
| Followers | 500–10,000 | Active enough to be real, small enough to reply |
| Upload frequency | 1+ track per month | They are working — they need beats |
| Label status | No major label affiliation | Independent = they need producers |
| Comments on tracks | Active comment sections | Real, engaged listeners |
| Bio language | "Available for features", "booking info", links to socials | Shows they treat music as a priority |

Search method: go to SoundCloud search, filter by "Tracks" with genre tags matching yours (deep house, dancehall). Listen to the vocals. If an artist's vocal style would fit your beats, note their profile.

**TikTok**

Look for artists who post original content (not covers of mainstream songs). Key signals:
- They post their own lyrics/melodies
- Captions like "wrote this today" or "in the studio"
- Under 50K followers (larger artists have teams and handlers)
- Comments on their posts where people are tagging others — indicates real engagement

**Instagram**

| Criteria | Target |
|----------|--------|
| Followers | 1,000–50,000 |
| Post frequency | Weekly or more |
| Content type | Original music clips, studio posts, not just lifestyle |
| Engagement rate | Above 3% (likes + comments / followers) — under 3% = bought followers likely |

Instagram search method: search hashtags like #deephousevocalist, #dancehallartist, #singersongwriter + your city or country. Filter for recent posts to find active accounts.

**YouTube**

Search: "[your genre] artist" or "[your subgenre] vocalist". Filter by "This month" under upload date. Channels with 100–10,000 subscribers and consistent uploads are the target. No major label link in the description.

**SubmitHub**

SubmitHub is a platform where artists submit their music to blogs and playlists. Many artists on SubmitHub are actively seeking producer collaborations, not just press. You can also find artists through the platform's directory and reach out directly — they are already in the mindset of seeking collaborators.

## 2. First Contact Scripts

### Script 1: Cold on SoundCloud (no prior engagement)

```
Hey [Name] — just came across your track "[song name]" and the vocal style really stood out.
I produce [genre] and I have something that I think would work well with your sound.
Would you want me to send a link to have a listen? No pressure at all.
```

Key: mention a specific song. Generic openers get ignored. Specific mentions show you actually listened.

### Script 2: Warm (after engaging for 1 week)

```
Hey [Name] — I've been following your music since your "[song name]" upload.
Good stuff. I produce [genre] and I've actually been working on something that
reminds me of your vibe. Would you want to hear it?
```

This works because you have context — you appeared in their notifications several times before reaching out. You are not a stranger.

### Script 3: Via Instagram DM

```
[Name] your sound is exactly what I've been looking for to put a beat to.
I make [genre] beats and I have one that fits your style — want me to drop the link?
```

Keep Instagram DMs very short. Long DMs on Instagram feel like spam. One line pitch, one ask.

### Script 4: Via email (if they list it publicly)

Subject line: `Beat for [Artist Name] — [Genre]`

```
Hi [Name],

I make [genre] beats and I came across your music on [platform].
Your track "[song name]" caught my attention — the way you handle [specific element, e.g. the hook / the low register / the energy shift] is something I rarely hear.

I've been working on a [genre] beat that I think would work well for your style.
No strings attached — I'd love to get your thoughts on it.

If you're open to it, I can send the link your way.

[Your name]
[Your BeatStars/SC link]
```

Email allows more context. Subject line should be personal — "[Artist Name]" in the subject line increases open rate significantly.

### Script 5: At a show or open mic (in person)

The in-person approach is the highest-conversion method but requires the artist to be local or at an event you are both attending.

Do not lead with "I have beats for you." Lead with a genuine compliment about their performance, ask about their music, find out what they are working on. Exchange details naturally. Follow up within 24 hours via DM referencing the conversation.

In-person intro:
```
"Your set was good — especially [specific song or moment].
What are you working on at the moment? Are you still producing your own stuff or looking for collabs?"
```

The question about production vs collabs opens the door naturally.

## 3. The Relationship Ladder

Every artist you engage with sits at a rung on this ladder. Your goal is to move each one up, not to rush to the bottom immediately.

```
Stranger
    │  (You find them, start following, listening)
    ▼
Engaged follower
    │  (You like and comment genuinely for 1–2 weeks)
    ▼
Commenter
    │  (They notice you, possibly comment back)
    ▼
DM acquaintance
    │  (First DM sent and received — conversation started)
    ▼
Beat recipient
    │  (You send them a beat — free or preview)
    ▼
Client
    │  (First paid license purchased)
    ▼
Repeat client
    │  (2+ purchases — they now know your sound and come back)
    ▼
Exclusive client
    │  (They buy exclusives, request customs, prioritize your catalog)
    ▼
Referral source
    (They recommend you to other artists without being asked)
```

Most producers try to jump from Stranger to Client in one DM. The relationship ladder is why the slow approach converts better — you are building trust at each rung.

## 4. Free Beat Strategy

### When to give a free beat — criteria

Not every artist deserves a free beat. A free beat is an investment. Give one when:

| Criterion | Threshold |
|-----------|----------|
| Audience size | 500+ genuine followers on any platform |
| Upload frequency | At least 1 release per month |
| Engagement quality | Real comments, not just bot likes |
| Genre match | Their vocal/lyrical style obviously fits your beats |
| Relationship status | At least DM acquaintance — not a cold stranger |

### What to include with a free beat

When you send a free beat, send it with context — not just a link.

```
Hey [Name] — here's that beat I mentioned: [SoundCloud unlisted link]

It's a free download for you to use. A couple of things:

• Please credit "Produced by BSP" (or [your name]) in the title/description if you upload it anywhere
• The free version is tagged (my voice tag plays at :30) — if you want the clean WAV version, just let me know and we can sort something out
• I'd love to hear what you do with it

Looking forward to it.
```

The structure: gift → credit ask (small) → hint at the upsell (natural, not pushy) → genuine interest in the output.

### How to follow up after sending a free beat

Timeline:

| Day | Action |
|-----|--------|
| Day of send | Send the beat with the message above |
| Day 3–5 | If no response: "Just checking you got this okay — SoundCloud links can sometimes go to spam" |
| Day 7 | If they opened it/used it: "Did you get a chance to hear it?" |
| Day 14 | If they uploaded it anywhere: "Saw you used the beat — it sounds great. Let me know if you want the clean version." (upsell trigger) |
| Day 30 | If nothing happened: move on. Keep them in your CRM with a note. Try again in 3 months with a different beat. |

### Tracking free beats that led to paid sales

Keep a record. Over time, patterns emerge — which type of artist converts, which platform's artists are more likely to buy, which genre of beat generates the most upsell requests.

Tracking fields: Artist name | Beat sent | Date | Platform | Free or tagged | Follow-up date | Outcome (used/not used, upsell happened/didn't, paid sale)

## 5. Client Retention

Getting a first sale is the beginning, not the goal. Repeat clients are far more valuable than new ones — they already trust you, already know your process, and require no sales effort.

### First sale follow-up (within 48 hours)

```
Hey [Name] — thanks for grabbing that license, it's appreciated.
If you ever want me to customize something for a specific project, just DM — I enjoy working with artists directly.
Looking forward to hearing what you do with it.
```

No upsell, no ask. Just warmth and an open door. This alone sets you apart from 95% of producers who never follow up at all.

### The "made something that fits" message

Every time you finish a new beat that genuinely reminds you of a past client's style, message them:

```
Hey [Name] — just finished a [genre] beat that reminded me of your sound on "[their song]".
Thought of you before I posted it anywhere. Want to hear it first?
```

"Thought of you before I posted it" is powerful. It makes them feel prioritized. Early access is a relationship reward.

### Exclusive early access system

Segment your BeatStars email list (or your external email list) so that past buyers get a "new beat" email 24–48 hours before anyone else. This is a retention tool:

- Past buyers feel valued
- They get first pick of exclusives before they sell to someone else
- It creates a reason to stay subscribed to your list

### Birthday and milestone acknowledgment

If a client's song using your beat hits a milestone (1,000 plays on SoundCloud, gets on a playlist, gets featured somewhere) — acknowledge it:

```
Saw "[their song]" crossed 1K plays — that's great.
You did something with that beat I didn't expect. Good work.
```

Brief, genuine, specific. Most producers never do this. This is relationship equity.

## 6. The Producer-on-Call Model

A retainer arrangement is the highest-value client relationship. Instead of per-beat transactions, an artist pays a monthly fee for a certain number of beats per month, custom-made for them.

### When a retainer makes sense

An artist is ready for a retainer when:
- They are buying from you regularly (2+ leases in 60 days)
- They mention needing more beats or ask about custom pricing
- Their release schedule requires consistent new material
- They have a budget (not hobbyists — artists with management, label support, or steady income from music)

### Retainer pricing structure

| Tier | Monthly price | What's included | Note |
|------|--------------|----------------|------|
| Starter | $150–$200/month | 3 custom beats/month (WAV, stems) | Good for artists releasing regularly |
| Standard | $300–$400/month | 5 custom beats/month + 1 exclusive per month | Producer becomes their primary source |
| Premium | $500+/month | Unlimited custom beats, exclusives as needed, priority turnaround | Full producer-on-call arrangement |

Custom beats for retainer clients are not taken from the public catalog — they are new beats made specifically for that artist's sound and current project.

### What to include in a retainer agreement

A retainer agreement should cover:

| Clause | Content |
|--------|---------|
| Duration | Month-to-month rolling, 30-day notice to cancel |
| Deliverables | Number of beats per month, format (WAV, stems), turnaround time |
| Ownership | You retain rights to the underlying composition; artist gets exclusive-use license for the month's beats |
| Credit | "Produced by [BSP/Your Name]" required on all releases |
| Revisions | X number of free revisions per beat included |
| Payment | First payment before work begins; monthly on the same date |
| Unused beats | Whether unused monthly beats roll over or expire |

Use BeatStars' contract generator as a base, then add the retainer-specific clauses. For any agreement above $300/month, consider having it reviewed by a music attorney once — a template legal review is a one-time cost that protects all future agreements.

## 7. Artist CRM in Notion (Free)

A CRM (Customer Relationship Management) system for artists does not need to be expensive software. A Notion database works perfectly and is free.

### Database structure

| Field | Type | Notes |
|-------|------|-------|
| Artist name | Text | Full name and any alias |
| Platform | Select | SoundCloud / Instagram / TikTok / YouTube / In Person |
| Profile URL | URL | Link to their main profile |
| Follower count | Number | Update quarterly |
| Genre | Select | Deep House / Dancehall / Hip-Hop / etc. |
| Relationship stage | Select | Stranger / Follower / DM Acquaintance / Beat Recipient / Client / Repeat Client / Retainer |
| Last contact | Date | When you last messaged them |
| Free beats sent | Number | Running count |
| Paid purchases | Number | Running count of transactions |
| Total revenue | Number | Cumulative spend from this artist |
| Next action | Text | What to do next (follow up / send beat / wait) |
| Notes | Text | Any context, specific preferences, project they mentioned |

### Weekly CRM review habit (10 minutes)

Every Monday:
1. Open the CRM
2. Filter by "Last contact" older than 14 days — who needs a follow-up?
3. Filter by "Relationship stage = Beat Recipient" — who has had a beat for 7+ days without feedback?
4. Filter by "Paid purchases ≥ 1 AND Last contact > 30 days" — past clients you have not touched in a month
5. Send 3–5 follow-ups or re-engagement messages based on those filters

The CRM review turns a vague sense of "I should reach out to someone" into a specific, prioritized list. It removes the mental overhead of remembering everyone.

## 8. Getting Artist Testimonials

Testimonials are the highest-converting form of social proof for beat sellers. An artist saying "BSP's beats are next-level" in a video converts skeptical buyers faster than any marketing copy you write.

### How to ask for a testimonial

Time the ask correctly — immediately after a positive interaction, not cold.

Triggering moments for asking:
- They thank you after receiving a beat
- Their song using your beat performed well
- They purchase a second or third license

Script:

```
Hey [Name] — glad the beat worked out for you.
Would you be open to leaving a quick review on my BeatStars profile? Even two lines.
It helps other artists find my work and I'd really appreciate it.
```

For a video testimonial (higher value):

```
Hey [Name] — this is a bigger ask, but would you be willing to record a quick 20-second video
saying what you thought of working with me? Can literally be on your phone, no production needed.
I'd use it on Instagram and my website. Happy to shout out your next release in exchange.
```

The exchange offer (shoutout for testimonial) makes it feel fair and worth their time.

### Where to display testimonials

| Platform | How to display |
|----------|---------------|
| BeatStars profile | Request reviews through BeatStars' built-in review feature |
| Instagram Highlights | Create a "Reviews" Highlight reel with screenshot testimonials |
| Instagram Reels | Post video testimonials as Reels (with artist permission) |
| SoundCloud profile | Quote a testimonial in your profile description |
| Carrd website | Add a testimonials section with quotes and artist names |
| YouTube | Add a testimonials montage to the start of a channel trailer or about video |

Video testimonials on Instagram Reels are the most impactful format — they are shareable, relatable, and can be discovered by strangers. A genuine artist reaction to hearing your beat for the first time, posted as a Reel, is worth more than any paid advertisement.
